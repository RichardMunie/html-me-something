The Early Stages

It all began with inspiration from a primitive fiddle I had seen in a violin shop in Saint Louis, Mo. The violin was rough, primitive and had a face if I remember correctly. A girl I was crushing on went there to buy an upgrade from her first violin. Being fond of taking my fiddle down to the river and wanting her to have a fiddle that she could take as well, without worrying about damaging a pricey instrument, I figured I would just carve out a primitive instrument like the one I saw on the wall at the shop. From there it started with an oak log.

The log I had procured from a friend who was in the tree felling business, a nice oak log. Armed with some primitive tools I began to hew away at the log, intent to shape it into a turtle which I happened to know the young lady fancied. It was hot that summer, the mosquitoes were ruthless and unrelenting. I would sometimes take the work into the basement just to avoid thier assault.

Taking Shape

still using primitive tools I began the arduous task of hollowing out the inside of the log. This is the point I called the fruit bowl stage for reasons readily apparent. I figured if anything I could have a fruit bowl. By this time the muse for which I toiled to bestow the fruit of this labor had begun seeing a deusche bag but I was undetered. The work had become a meditation for me of sorts and a vision was forming in my head. New elements in the design stirred in my head as I hammered away at the chunck of oak. I had consulted at one point a luthier, looking for a fingerboard. He told me I didn't want to use oak, it was too heavy a wood and would not produce any sound. I also had shown my work to a carver who had sharpened a gouge for me. He said I didn't want to use oak, it would split on me. It had, I glued it. Needless to say, I ignored any expert advice that was given.

At one point I realized that there is a reason that violins are waisted. That means the go in at the middle of the bodies kind of like the waist of a woman. The purpose of waisting is so that the bow is free to attack the strings at an angle in order to engage a single string. This is about time I started to give the turtle shell details and I now had to bring in the waist even though I had already begun to hollow it out. Had I hollowed it out to much? This is where forethought and designing ahead of execution might have saved me from some worry but I forged on ahead and things began to take shape.
